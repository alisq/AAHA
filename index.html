DEMAND 01

Indigenous Housing on Urban Land

Region: West Coast


Each team will develop a demand and architectural proposal that will be placed here.

Housing development designed by Patrick Stewart


This team will focus on the distinct and urgent needs of culturally appropriate and adequate housing for urban Indigenous people in Canada. The team will draw on traditional Indigenous knowledge to produce new morphologies, financial and social formations in housing.


TEAM BIOGRAPHIES


Activist

Chief Ian Campbell

Chief Ian Campbell is a Hereditary Chief of the Squamish Nation who served 16 years as an Elected Councillor and 22 years as a Lead Negotiator for the Squamish Nation. He has an MBA from Simon Fraser University and has been instrumental in major land acquisitions, land development, industrial projects, forestry, and environmental protection. He is a traditional knowledge keeper, versed in the history and mythology of the Squamish Nation. Chief Campbell works as a strategic advisor on many land development projects providing transformative changes that counter erasure of Indigenous Peoples, while creating tangible acts of reconciliation.


Architect

Patrick Stewart Architect

Patrick R. Stewart Architect is a full-service architectural firm, with a First Nations community development focus. Patrick was the first architect of First Nations ancestry in B.C. to own and operate an architectural firm in B.C. Prominent projects include the David Pranteau Aboriginal Children’s Village in Vancouver, which provides housing for foster children and their families.


Advocate

Sarah Silva, Hiy̓ám̓ ta Housing

Sarah Silva is the CEO of Hiy̓ám̓ ta Housing, an incorporated non-profit society, newly formed by the Squamish Nation Council as part of its strategic priority to house every Squamish member within a generation. The society’s purpose is to provide affordable housing for low-to-moderate income families with a priority, but not exclusive, focus on Squamish Nation members, all in a manner consistent with Squamish Nation values and snewiyelh (Squamish laws).

DEMAND 02

Hybrid Forms of Collective Ownership

Region: West Coast


Each team will develop aand architectural proposal that will be placed here.

Haecity Missing Middle


This team will focus on the dwindling supply of Single Room Occupancy (SRO) units and how a meaningful response can be generated through combining the ownership structures of cohousing and cooperatives with the morphologies of high urban density.


TEAM BIOGRAPHIES


Activist

Right to Remain

Right to Remain is a research project that examines the Single Room Occupancy (SRO) buildings in Vancouver’s Downtown Eastside. The intensifying pace of SRO conversions from low-income to middle-class housing in the Vancouver Downtown Eastside mirrors previous rounds of creative destruction inflicted on this community through colonialism, racial prejudice, and the withdrawal of care for the mentally ill, veterans, and others who have found refuge here. Together with the activist group SRO Collective, they are organizing tenants to create politics of inhabitation that work against neoliberal forces of dislocation, and pursuing direct action and advocacy for the tenants.


Architect

Haeccity Studio Architecture

Founded in 2011 by Shirley Shen and Travis Hanks, Haeccity Studio Architecture is committed to practicing responsible design and realizing lasting structures for the healthy integration of human and natural systems. Our female BIPOC led team is fortunate to conduct our research through built projects, advocacy, and teaching on the unceded territories of the Squamish, Musqueum and Tsleil-Waututh Nations. Haeccity has been featured in numerous publications and is the recipient of many awards, including a City of Vancouver Heritage Award of Merit in 2019, and Urbanarium’s First and Planner’s Prizes for the 2018 Missing Middle Competition.


Advocate

Canadian Cohousing Network

The Canadian Cohousing Network (CCN) was formed in 1992 in British Columbia, Canada. It is a registered non-profit organization that promotes the creation of cohousing communities as a model for sustainable development by raising public awareness about cohousing and by bringing people together to form communities. The most valuable function of the CCN is making connections with people who are interested in living in a cohousing community. CCN links individuals and cohousing groups together to share resources and make the process of creating a community easier and more economical. The purpose of the CCN is to: promote and facilitate the development of cohousing communities across Canada; foster public, government, and building professions’ awareness about the nature and benefits of cohousing; provide educational services for creating cohousing communities; enable networking opportunities for those interested in cohousing; establish standards for cohousing development including accreditation of cohousing professionals; maintain a cohousing resource directory; and provide support services to groups involved in creating cohousing communities. The Society operates without object of gain for its members or directors. Any profits or other accretions will only be used in promoting its objectives. These provisions are unalterable.

The Right to Housing on Reserve

Region: Prairies


Each team will develop a demand and architectural proposal that will be placed here.

Round Prarie Elders Lodge in Saskatoon designed by David T. Fortin


Breaking the cycle of on-reserve housing as an incongruent and alienated product, detached from both cultural and economic relevance, as well as inferior overall performance, requires a systemic reconceptualization that is led by Indigenous communities and designers.


TEAM BIOGRAPHIES


Activist

Idle No More

Idle No More's One House Many Nations (OHMN) initiative works to address the dire housing situation on First Nations and other Indigenous communities. This initiative has brought much-needed attention and awareness to the housing crisis and homelessness that affects Canadians and Indigenous peoples, while making a difference by building housing and supports for land defense. The Muskrat Hut project was a finalist in the Team category in the Science, Technology, Innovation and Collaboration Award in 2019. Most recently, One House Many Nations' Making the Shift research is working with youth in four First Nations to build four homes on-reserve with and for the homelessness in these communities.


Architect

David T. Fortin Architect

David T. Fortin Architect Inc. is a small architectural practice based in Sudbury, Ontario that works primarily with Métis and First Nations communities across Canada on various residential and cultural projects. The firm was established in 2018 and has built a reputation for their emphasis on listening closely to community needs and desires as their first priority. They have also worked with organizations such as the Gabriel Dumont Institute in Saskatoon, the Métis Nation of Saskatchewan, and the Assembly of First Nations, and also collaborate with various firms on public projects where there is an emphasis on Indigenous community engagement and design.


Advocate

Shawn Bailey and Lancelot Coar

Lancelot Coar is an Associate Professor and researcher at the Centre for Architectural Structures and Technology at the University of Manitoba. For the past eight years, this work has been carried out in collaboration with several First Nations partners to work towards achieving design sovereignty and address the severe housing shortage and substandard quality of homes in these communities. Shawn Bailey is an Indigenous scholar and Assistant Professor at the University of Manitoba, and practicing architect with over 15 years of experience working professionally and academically with Indigenous communities. Through community collaboration, he explores housing solutions that build capacity by examining processes rooted in narrative. The focus is to build relationships with the community and the land through actions grounded in reciprocity.

DEMAND 04

Remote Housing for an Indigenous Majority

Region: Northern Territories


Each team will develop a demand and architectural proposal that will be placed here.


Home is Land. In the north one can be house-less while still being “at home”. As economic development follows resource extraction throughout the region, where commodity prices remain higher than in other places, housing continues to be a major challenge. How can the intimate relationship to the Land offer a different path forward?


TEAM BIOGRAPHIES


Activist

The Arctic Indigenous Wellness Foundation (AIWF)

The Arctic Indigenous Wellness Foundation (AIWF) has brought together in a symbolic self-determined union, the Inuit, Dene, and Métis with the mandate of culturally reviving traditional healing services and practices in Canada’s north. AIWF is a non-profit organization dedicated to improving traditional and culturally based health care for indigenous northerners facing a high burden of disease and unequal access to traditional health services. AIWF is committed to working with patients, health workers, researchers, and policymakers to ensure equal access for all northerners to traditionally based quality wellness services.


Architect

Ouri Scott Architect

Ouri Scott is a Tlicho Dene architect originally from the Northwest Territories, currently living and practicing in Vancouver, BC. Ouri acts as an advocate and instigator, drawing on her roots to bring indigenous perspectives to projects, including advising on process and protocols, developing culturally appropriate engagement approaches and innovative design strategies. Ouri works closely with clients to give form to their goals, developing a design language that is responsive to contemporary Indigenous culture and values for housing, health care, community and advanced education projects. Ouri is an active member of the RAIC Indigenous Task Force. She has spoken at conferences and community events in Canada and New Zealand on indigenous design and sustainable infrastructure development.


Advocate

Julia Christensen

Julia Christensen is a Canada Research Chair in Northern Governance and Public Policy in the Department of Geography at Memorial University, and holds a PhD in Geography from McGill University. She is settler Canadian, and was born and raised in the City of Yellowknife on Chief Drygeese Territory in Denendeh (Northwest Territories). Dr. Christensen has worked with themes of housing insecurity, home and homelessness, urbanization and health in collaboration with northern communities, governments and NGOs for the past 12 years. In 2016, she co-edited the book Indigenous Homelessness: Perspectives from Canada, Australia and New Zealand, and in 2017, published her book No Home in a Homeland: Indigenous Peoples and Homelessness in the Canadian North (UBC Press).

TEAM BIOGRAPHIES



Activist

Parkdale Neighbourhood Land Trust (PNLT)

Parkdale Neighbourhood Land Trust (PNLT) is a not-for-profit community land trust in Parkdale led by a group of residents and organizations trying to protect the social, cultural and economic diversity of Parkdale by redefining how land is used and developed. Through the community land trust model, PNLT owns land and assures it is used to meet the needs of the community by leasing it to non-profit partners who can provide affordable housing, furnish spaces for social enterprises and non-profit organizations, and offer urban agriculture and open space. Also, PNLT promotes community participation in guiding how land is used to benefit the community and keep it affordable and diverse. PNLT is currently run by the board of directors that has been elected by our members and consists of residents and representatives of local organizations that represent the diversity of Parkdale.


Architect

LGA Architectural Partners

LGA Architectural Partners is a skilled team of architects and building scientists based out of Toronto, who create sustainable, contextually-sensitive and socially-minded spaces and places. Since 1989, we have been designing spaces that reflect the highest ideals and aspirations of our clients, while contributing to the healthy growth and vibrancy of the communities we serve. We aspire to make open architecture—one that can catalyze cultural and economic progress through the creation of inviting public spaces that encourage interaction. Our research-based process involves engaging many perspectives, each enhancing the breadth and quality of our work. We tell the unique story of a place and its people, through the careful shaping of space, and the resourceful detailing of materials. Design is an essential tool for improving people’s lives, and we believe that all architecture should be intrinsically sustainable, beautiful, and built to adapt to the needs of future generations.


Advocate

Gentrification Tax Action

Gentrification Tax Action (GTA) is a collective of artists and designers formed to campaign for a hypothecated tax that would directly target processes of gentrification in Toronto and help build affordable housing. This tax would be levied as a percentage of the increase in home value, declining from the year of purchase, in order to: 1) disincentivize speculation and the displacement of low income residents, 2) incentivize rental housing, as owners are forced to rent while waiting for the tax to decline, 3) keep the social increment of land profit generated by the community in the neighbourhood, and 4) fund deeply affordable local housing in the form of cooperatives and land trusts. Current members include: artist and documentary filmmaker Sameer Farooq, artist Kika Thorne, artist and urbanist Adrian Blackwell, and landscape architect Jane Mah Hutton.

Evolutionary Housing for Tent Encampments

Region: Ontario


Each team will develop a demand and architectural proposal that will be placed here.

some caption text here


The team has experience supporting individuals living in tent encampments. It will design an architectural strategy that gives individuals living rough, or in tents, a foothold in secure housing, that can then provide a platform for housing evolution, allowing residents the agency to transform and expand their space to address their changing living situations.


TEAM BIOGRAPHIES


Activist

The Encampment Support Network (ESN)

The Encampment Support Network (ESN) is an ad-hoc, volunteer-run network supporting people living in encampments in 6 locations throughout Toronto. This includes ESN Parkdale, ESN Trinity Bellwoods, ESN Scadding Court, ESN Moss Park, ESN LNP and ESN Cherry Beach. Outreach volunteers from our network visit encampments daily to deliver basic supplies such as water and tents, ice, sleeping bags, fire safety equipment, and snacks, which the city has consistently refused to provide. We believe all people deserve care, support, and access to water, food, fire safety and sanitation. We advocate for better conditions in encampments, report on city conditions and activity in encampments, and advocate for long term permanent housing for people in their communities of choice. ESN also collects and compiles feedback from residents to support our advocacy efforts and continues to pressure the city to develop real solutions to the housing crisis. The only way to provide effective support and find solutions is by listening to and centring the needs of people experiencing homelessness.


Architect

SvN Architects + Planners

John van Nostrand FRAIC, FCIP is the Founding Partner in SvN Architects + Planners. Since 1978, he has been the driving force behind the firm’s architectural, planning, and urban design practices. Throughout, SvN has focused on the integration of housing, land development, and infrastructure in cities and landscapes undergoing constant growth and renewal. In 2018, he created Parcel Developments to focus on evolutionary housing and its application to entry-level housing for all incomes. John’s work and writings have been well recognized at home and abroad, and his awards include the World Leadership Award for Town Planning (1992), the Daniel Burnham Award from the American Planning Association (2008) and numerous local and national Awards of Excellence in Architecture, Planning, and Urban Design. In 2004, John was awarded the Jane Jacobs Award for “Ideas That Matter” and is the recent recipient of the OAA’s Order of da Vinci Award in recognition of his exceptional leadership in the profession, and the education of, and service to its communities.


Advocate

Toronto Tiny Shelters

Toronto Tiny Shelters is a volunteer fabrication collective, formed in September 2020 to respond to the houselessness crisis in Toronto. With the municipal emergency shelter system at capacity due to the Coronavirus pandemic, houseless individuals in Toronto began seeking refuge in tents in ravines and public parks. As winter approached, local carpenter Khaleel Sievwright and a team of volunteer builders began raising funds to build temporary insulated structures as warm and safe alternatives to tents for anyone living outdoors. Through a public GoFundMe and in cooperation with community members, Toronto Tiny Shelters delivered over one hundred shelters to houseless individuals in less than four months.

Activist

Le Front d’action populaire en réaménagement urbain

Le Front d’action populaire en réaménagement urbain est un regroupement national pour le droit au logement. Le FRAPRU est aussi actif sur les enjeux d’aménagement urbain, de lutte contre la pauvreté et de promotion des droits sociaux. Il est présentement composé de quelque 140 groupes membres. Certains de ces groupes sont ce qu’on appelle des membres participants. Ils sont au cœur du travail du FRAPRU. Ils définissent les points de vue et les activités et voient à leur réalisation. Ils sont actuellement au nombre de 30. Les autres groupes sont des membres associés. Ils ne sont pas nécessairement impliqués directement dans les interventions et les actions du FRAPRU, mais ils les appuient dans la mesure de leurs moyens. Le FRAPRU a été fondé en 1978, on peut en savoir plus sur son histoire en lisant la brochure publiée pour son 40ème anniversaire, ou en regardant un documentaire produit pour la même occasion: https://www.frapru.qc.ca/40-ans-au-front/

Advocate

Bâtir son quartier

Bâtir son quartier est une entreprise d’économie sociale qui accompagne les organismes et les ménages à faible ou modeste revenu dans la prise en charge collective de leurs conditions de logement et la création de milieux de vie solidaires. Pour ce faire, elle coordonne la réalisation de projets d’habitation et d’immobilier communautaire, développés principalement selon des formules coopératives ou à but non lucratif. Depuis le début de ses interventions en 1976, Bâtir son quartier a coordonné la réalisation de 14 310 logements communautaires dans le cadre de 450 projets répartis sur l’ensemble du territoire de la région métropolitaine de Montréal, en plus de nombreux projets de centres de la petite enfance (CPE), de centres communautaires et d’installations pour des organismes communautaires et des entreprises collectives.

Activist

Brique par Brique

Brique par Brique is a non-profit organization created in 2016 by community organizers and professionals of color in order to innovatively respond to needs in affordable housing in the rapidly gentrifying and multicultural neighborhood of Parc-Extension, through the lens of social justice. Their mission is to foster living spaces that nurture mutual engagement, facilitate empowerment, and prioritize low-income residents that face discrimination in the rental market. Architect

Anne Cormier

Anne Cormier has a professional bachelor’s degree in architecture from McGill University (1982) and a Certificat d’études approfondies en architecture urbaine from the Paris-Villemin school of architecture (1987), where she wrote a dissertation on development projects and the transformation of Paris in the early 20th century. Activist

Halifax Mutual Aid Society

Halifax Mutual Aid Society is a group of people who are deeply concerned that the city of Halifax and the province of Nova Scotia choose to ignore houselessness. They stand by while people are left in the cold without access to the most basic shelter. We are tired of these local governments that pretend “the housing crisis” that they created is impossible to solve. We are tired of these local governments who break their own laws and bylaws to allow luxury development that is inaccessible to the average Halifax resident. We are tired of seeing municipal and publicly-owned property being left empty and derelict or sold off to private developers instead of being retrofitted for social housing. In response to the inadequacy, corruption, and incompetence of municipal and provincial officials to solve this crisis, we have chosen to provide some form of shelter to the houseless directly. We will build small, watertight, and insulated shelters for anyone stuck in the cold this winter. We do this because otherwise residents of Halifax will die.


Architect

Dr. Susan Fitzgerald is the Design Director and Managing Partner at FBM. She is also an assistant professor at Dalhousie University. Her design work has been the recipient of many accolades including the Canada Council for the Arts Professional Prix de Rome, Canadian Architect Awards, Governor General's Medal in Architecture, the Wood Design Award, the EnRoute Air Canada Award in partnership with the RAIC, Maritime Design Awards, several Lieutenant Governor's Awards including the Medal of Excellence, and SABMag Green Building of the Year Award. Advocate

Affordable Housing Association of Nova Scotia

The Affordable Housing Association of Nova Scotia (AHANS) has been actively involved in building affordable housing and in upgrading, protecting and better utilizing the province's existing housing stock since the mid-1980’s. We are active learners and teachers who gladly share our expertise through housing research, education, and consulting services. Furthermore, since 2012 AHANS has been the Community Entity for Service Canada’s Reaching Home Homelessness funding program. We aspire to be a non-profit that is resilient, with a housing portfolio that meets the current and changing housing needs. Further, we believe that locally led responses lead to better economic, education and health outcomes. AHANS values respectful, mutually beneficial partnerships with the private sector and non-profit housing providers, and government agencies. We evaluate our activities on our version of the triple bottom line – social, environmental and financial outcomes.
